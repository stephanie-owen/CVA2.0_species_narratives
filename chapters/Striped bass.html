38.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

Coutant (1990) analyzed habitat suitability for striped bass on the U.S. east coast with predicted temperature increases and declines in precipitation. In freshwater areas, high temperatures, altered streamflow, and low oxygen may constrain suitable habitat in the summer. Habitat loss in the Chesapeake Bay is predicted, with a possible range expansion into Nova Scotia and further into the Gulf of St. Lawrence (Coutant 1990). Onset and cessation of the spawning season in two spawning grounds of the Hudson River Estuary have occurred earlier in recent years. Spawning sites in the upper estuary are more vulnerable to climate change compared to sites in the lower estuary (Pan et al. 2023). Larval striped bass abundance in the Hudson River Estuary is positively correlated with high freshwater flows, which may decrease under predicted climate scenarios (O’Connor et al. 2012). Striped bass recruitment has been linked to winter North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) and marine heatwaves (Dixon et al. 2026). Several studies support a general conclusion that dominant year classes through recent history have always been preceded by a colder than normal winter (i.e. subnormal winter temperatures). One proposed mechanism of the severe winter-strong year class association is through the estuarine food chain (Heinle and Flemer 1975) (Heinle et al. 1977).

38.8 Life History Synopsis:

Atlantic striped bass inhabit freshwater coastal and estuarine waters from Canada to Florida, including the Gulf of America from Florida to Louisiana (Northeast Fisheries Science Center 2019). Striped bass are anadromous, spawning once a year in fresh or nearly fresh water. Along the Atlantic coast, the spawning period ranges from mid-February in Florida to June and July in the Gulf of St. Lawrence. In the mid-Atlantic, spawning is concentrated during the months of April - June (Bigelow and Schroeder 1953). Spawning peaks are apparently triggered by a noticeable increase in water temperature, generally beginning at temperatures of at least 14°C (Johnson and Koo 1975). Eggs hatch within 30 - 74 hrs, occurring faster at higher temperatures. Larval stages last up until age 1 when juveniles migrate out of rivers and into estuaries (Northeast Fisheries Science Center 2019). Larvae and eggs may drift down stream in rivers, away from spawning sites. Eggs are semi-buoyant, requiring a current of 30 cm/sec in order to keep the eggs afloat so they don’t settle to the bottom and die (Fay et al. 1983). Egg and larval mortality, as well as larval growth rates and yolk utilization are all linked to water temperatures greater than 18°C (Northeast Fisheries Science Center 2019). Predation and competition by American shad are important factors in spawning and larval locations (Fay et al. 1983). In addition, adult and juvenile white perch and copepod Cyclops bicuspidatus probably consume large numbers of striped bass larvae (Smith and Kernehan 1981). Larval striped bass feed on zooplankton; juveniles eat small shrimps and other crustaceans, annelid worms, bay anchovies, gobies, and insects (Bason 1971). Most juveniles remain in the river and estuarine areas where they were spawned. During the first summer, young-of-the-year striped bass generally move downstream and shoreward (Fay et al. 1983). A variety of environmental factors influence the timing of this migration, including temperature, pH, and salinity (Northeast Fisheries Science Center 2019). Males reach maturity at age 2-4 and females from age 4-8. Striped bass have a fairly long lifespan, up to 30 years (Northeast Fisheries Science Center 2019). Adults are generalists, consuming a variety of fish and invertebrates, depending on fish size, time of year, and habitat (Northeast Fisheries Science Center 2019). Adults have few predators, with the exception of some seals and sharks (Northeast Fisheries Science Center 2019). The Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission manages striped bass in coastal and estuarine waters from Maine to Virginia, and coastal areas of North Carolina under Amendment 7 of the Interstate Fishery Management Plan (Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission 2022). Estuarine waters of North Carolina are managed by the state. The stock is currently overfished, with management measures and a rebuilding plan in place, but not subject to overfishing (Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission 2024).
