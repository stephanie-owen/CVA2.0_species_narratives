29.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

Radiocarbon aging on ocean quahog shells suggest past (200 years) ocean quahog distributions inshore of their current range, indicating a range shift coinciding with the regression of the Cold Pool after the Little Ice Age (LeClaire et al. 2022). With settlement occurring at the end of the larval stage, it may be presumed that temperature impacts settlement rate. Weinberg et al. (2002) note that an increase in temperature would shift the species offshore and north. Future warming conditions are predicted to result in range shifts in Atlantic surfclams that would lead to potential competition for food and space between surfclams and ocean quahogs (Stromp et al. 2023) (Spencer et al. 2024).

29.8 Life History Synopsis:

Ocean quahogs are bivalve mollusks that inhabit the Northeast U.S. Continental Shelf from Newfoundland to Cape Hatteras (Cargnelli et al. 1999). The ocean quahog is among the longest-lived and slowest growing of marine bivalves, reaching maturity around 13 years of age and harvestable size at age 20 (Cargnelli et al. 1999). Maximum ages have been documented as high as 507 years old, with ages over 300 years old more common in the Northwest Atlantic (Sower et al. 2023). Spawning occurs either in summer or fall, and can extend for months, releasing eggs and sperm into the water column at low levels over time (Cargnelli et al. 1999). Multiple annual spawnings may occur at the individual and population levels (Mann 1982). Eggs and larvae drift with currents until metamorphosis, then settle to the bottom (Mid-Atlantic Fishery Management Council (MAFMC) 1997). Larval development, at minimum, lasts for 55 days and decreases with increasing temperature (Lutz et al. 1981). Larvae go through three stages of development (trochophores, veligers and pediveligers) with the duration of each stage being temperature dependent (Cargnelli et al. 1999). Van de Water Lewis (1997) noted that wind-forced circulation may play a significant role in planktonic and benthic recruitment on Georges Bank and that interannual differences in these winds can yield very different abundance patterns over the bank. Once settled to the bottom, juvenile ocean quahogs are found offshore in sandy substrates, but may survive in muddy intertidal environments if protected from predators (Kraus et al. 1989). Cross-shelf distributions of juveniles and adults remain within a 10°C optimal temperature range, as temperatures above 16°C limit distribution (Cargnelli et al. 1999). Ocean quahogs are suspension feeders on phytoplankton, using their relatively short siphons which are extended above the surface of the substrate to pump in water (Cargnelli et al. 1999). Juvenile and small ocean quahogs are commonly preyed upon by crustaceans (rock crabs, sea stars) and fish (haddock, cod) (Cargnelli et al. 1999). Ocean quahogs are managed by the Mid-Atlantic Fishery Management Council under the Atlantic Surfclam-Ocean Quahog Fishery Management Plan. The species is mainly harvested off of Maine and Long Island. The last stock assessment was conducted in 2019, where the stock was not overfished and overfishing was not occurring (Mid-Atlantic Fishery Management Council (MAFMC) 2025).
