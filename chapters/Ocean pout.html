28.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

Nye et al. (2009) identified an increase in depth of occurrence of ocean pout in the Gulf of Maine. Although ocean pout have been reported to be quite hardy, they appear to have limited tolerance of hypoxic and anoxic conditions; they are common victims of these conditions which occasionally occur in the New York Bight (Azarovitz et al. 1976). Ocean pout are sluggish swimmers, which can explain their inability to avoid stressful conditions, such as hypoxia (Steimle et al. 1999). Killen et al. (2008) found that young ocean pout from Newfoundland do not show thermal compensation in response to long-term temperature changes.

28.8 Life History Synopsis:

Ocean pout are a non-migratory, bottom dwelling fish found along the Atlantic coast from Labrador to Delaware (Steimle et al. 1999). Spawning occurs in the late summer through early winter (peak in September-October) with earlier peaks (August-October) in the south (Wilk and Morse 1979). Eggs are laid in gelatinous masses in a sheltered place on the bottom, such as rocky crevices, where they are guarded either by one or both parents until hatching (Steimle et al. 1999). Eggs develop in 2-3 months, temperature dependent (Olsen and Merriman 1946). Larvae are about 30 mm long at hatching (Clark and Livingstone 1982) and live at the bottom at temperatures below 10°C (Steimle et al. 1999). Juvenile and adult ocean pout use both open and rough habitats. Sedberry (1983) reported that juveniles were common during the summer on the sandy, mid- to outer-continental shelf (approximately 35-95 m). In the Gulf of Maine, juveniles occur in shallow coastal waters around rocks and attached algae, and in rivers with saline bottom waters (Auster 1985). MacDonald (1983) reported that ocean pout feed by sorting mouthfuls of sediments for infauna and do not use visual cues to stimulate feeding; they maintained that ocean pout were not very effective at capturing moving prey and rarely left the bottom to feed. Auster (1985) and Auster et al. (1995), however, considered ocean pout an “ambush predator” that waits in sediment depressions for prey to approach or drift by in the current. Juveniles feed primarily on gammarid amphipods and polychaetes (Sedberry 1983). Adult ocean pout feed on a variety of benthic invertebrates, including polychaetes, mollusks, crustaceans, and echinoderms (Steimle et al. 1999). Adults reach maturity at 2+ years (Steimle et al. 1999), with a maximum age of 18 years (Hoenig 1983). Although they do not migrate, ocean pout move seasonally within a region: offshore in late summer and inshore in the spring in the Gulf of Maine, and offshore to cooler waters in the summer returning inshore in the fall in Southern New England and Georges Bank (Steimle et al. 1999). Ocean pout are managed by the New England Fishery Management Council under the Northeast Multispecies Fishery Management Plan. As of the 2022 management track assessment, the stock is not overfished and overfishing is not occurring (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2022).
