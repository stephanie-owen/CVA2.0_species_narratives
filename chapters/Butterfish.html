19.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

Butterfish distributions and abundance in the Mid-Atlantic Bight are strongly related to bottom temperature (Kohut et al. 2013). Butterfish have shifted their distribution northward in recent decades. Collie et al. (2008) documented an increase in butterfish abundance in Narragansett Bay in the latter half of the 20th century, and they recently have expanded their range into the Gulf of Maine (Suca et al. 2021) (Adams 2022). Their depth distribution has also shifted to inhabiting deeper waters (Suca et al. 2021). An increase in butterfish prevalence in the Gulf of Maine has altered seabird diets, and is replacing preferred prey in common terns (Smith and Craig 2023).

19.8 Life History Synopsis:

Butterfish distribution ranges from Newfoundland in Canada to Atlantic Florida, but they are primarily found from the Gulf of Maine to Cape Hatteras. They are a schooling fish and shift distribution in response to changing water temperatures: moving north and inshore over the summer to spawn and south and offshore in the winter to seek warmer water (Cross et al. 1999). Butterfish are broadcast spawners and spawn primarily in the evening or at night, however no direct observations have been made (Horn 1970). In general, butterfish spawn starting in May through early fall, peaking in June-July, although timing is dependent on location (Cross et al. 1999). Egg duration and larval growth is temperature dependent (Colton and Honey 1963), and most eggs and larvae prefer temperatures 9-19°C (Cross et al. 1999). Butterfish eggs and larvae are widely distributed throughout their range from spring to fall as spawning occurs over their entire range. Larvae are associated with the Gulf Stream, although it is not clear how far they travel (Morse et al. 1987). Later life stages (juveniles and adults) can inhabit surface waters to depth on the continental shelf and into coastal bays and estuaries (Schreiber 1973). Survival is reduced in juveniles and adults below 10°C (Cross et al. 1999). Juvenile and adult butterfish feed mainly on planktonic prey including thaliaceans (primarily Larvacea and Hemimyaria), mollusks (primarily squids), crustaceans, coelenterates, polychaetes, small fishes, and ctenophores (Horn 1970). Recent diet analysis on 2013-2015 trawl specimens illustrates that spring diets are dominated by Calanus copepods, amphipods, larvaceans, and fall diets are dominated by amphipods (Suca et al. 2018). There is little information about larval predation; juvenile and adult butterfish are preyed upon by haddock, silver hake, goosefish, weakfish, bluefish, swordfish, sharks (hammerhead), and longfin inshore squid (Bigelow and Schroeder 1953) (Horn 1970). Butterfish age at maturity is 1-2 years; the maximum lifespan is 6 years although most butterfish live roughly 2-3 years (Horn 1970) (Draganik and Zukowski 1966). In the Northwest Atlantic, butterfish are managed by the Mid-Atlantic Fishery Management Council under the Mackerel, Squid, and Butterfish Fishery Management Plan. As of the 2024 stock assessment, the stock is not overfished and overfishing is not occurring (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2024).
