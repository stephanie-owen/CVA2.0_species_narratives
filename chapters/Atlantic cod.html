5.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

Recent warming in the Gulf of Maine relating to a northward shift in the Gulf Stream and changes in the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation and Pacific Decadal Oscillation led to reduced Atlantic cod recruitment and increased mortality (Pershing et al. 2015). Rising temperatures may affect Atlantic cod spawning locations and timing (Behan et al. 2023). Drinkwater (2005) suggested Atlantic cod spawning grounds will experience a shift northward, with spawning timing occurring later in the season as a response to warming ocean temperatures. Climate change may also increase in natural mortality in adult Atlantic cod due to declines in fish condition (Behan et al. 2023). Drinkwater (2005) predicted large-scale declines in Atlantic cod in the Gulf of Maine if bottom temperatures increase above 12°C, either through changes in distribution to colder waters or natural mortality. Reduced fitness in Atlantic cod adults from climate impacts may result in lower quality eggs (Klein et al. 2016); however, there may be some capacity to adapt to slower rates of warming (Langan et al. 2020).

5.8 Life History Synopsis:

Cod in the Northwest Atlantic range from Greenland to Cape Hatteras, NC, with a majority of biomass in the Gulf of Maine and on Georges Bank (Behan et al. 2023). Spawning timing varies by location - later in the year with increasing latitude - although peaks generally occur in the winter and spring (Lough 2004) in water temperatures between 5-7°C. In spring, Atlantic cod spawning matched the variation in peak abundance of Calanus finmarchicus. In fall, cod spawned in some, but not all, areas where Calanus were abundant. This is consistent with the hypothesis that cod spawning is coupled to copepod production (Brander and Hurley 1992). Atlantic cod eggs are pelagic and hatch after 8-60 days, varying with temperature (Hardy 1978). Recruitment success increases when dispersion of larvae by currents is reduced (Cong et al. 1996). On Georges Bank, a clockwise gyre circulation pattern tends to retain and isolate eggs and larvae (Lough and Bolz 1989). Transformation from larvae to the juvenile stage occurs at sizes greater than 20 mm, where they then move to the bottom (Lough 2004). Early juveniles are dependent on gravel substrates or grass beds in nearshore nurseries to favor survival through predator avoidance and increased food availability (Lough and Bolz 1989). In the Gulf of Maine, juveniles prey on mollusks and other invertebrates, while adults consume a greater proportion of fish including herring, redfish, mackerel, and other cod (Bowman 1975). Adult cod have few predators, other than large sharks and seals (Neuenhoff et al. 2019). Recent modeling studies illustrate growing populations of grey seals, harp seals, and dogfish are a major barrier to cod population recovery (Behan et al. 2023). Adults in the Newfoundland stock migrate, leaving overwintering areas in deep oceanic waters and follow tongues of deep, relatively warm, oceanic waters (“highways”) across the shelf to summer feeding areas nearshore. They then move northward along the Newfoundland coast in late summer, and eventually return to wintering areas (Lough 2004). Previous research suggested that in the middle part of their range, Atlantic cod only undertake minor seasonal movements in reaction to changing temperatures. However, more recent research suggests that there are inshore/offshore migrations that correspond to spawning and feeding activity in the Gulf of Maine (Zemeckis et al. 2017). As of 2025, Atlantic cod are divided into 4 stock units (Western & Eastern Gulf Maine, Georges Bank, and Southern New England) based on spawning locations, larval transport pathways, and genetic markers (New England Fishery Management Council (NEFMC) 2025). All stocks are managed under the New England Fishery Management Council’s Northeast Multispecies Fishery Management Plan (Klein-MacPhee 2012). The stock is overfished and overfishing is occurring in the Gulf of Maine (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2021).
