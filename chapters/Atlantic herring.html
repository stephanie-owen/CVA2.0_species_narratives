8.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

A number of Atlantic herring life history parameters are linked to drivers of climate change. Climate-related mismatches between Atlantic herring larval feeding and prey abundance have resulted in slower growth and higher mortality (Randall et al. 2022). Gröger et al. (2010) found that lags in the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) and Atlantic Multi-decadal Oscillation (AMO) were the primary drivers of Atlantic herring recruitment. Winter NAO has also indirectly influenced recruitment in multiple herring stocks (Axenrot and Hansson 2003) (Oskarsson and Taggart 2010) (Lynam et al. 2005) and may be used to predict recruitment with lead times up to 5 years (Gröger et al. 2010). Atlantic herring recruitment is also influenced by larval temperatures, the Gulf Stream Index, and food availability from water mass movement (Molina 2025). Favorable habitat for Atlantic herring in the Northwest Atlantic is currently decreasing with rising temperatures and bottom salinity (Suca et al. 2021), with herring populations in warmer waters responding more negatively to increasing temperatures than those in colder waters (Beaudry-Sylvestre et al. 2024). Herring larvae in the Baltic Sea are facing more heat stress and cardiac dysfunction with an increase in the number of marine heat waves (Gröger et al. 2024). Migration and distribution of Atlantic herring is also linked with thermal oceanic fronts between colder, less saline continental shelf water and warmer, more salient slope water (where plankton are also usually more abundant) (Kelly and Moring 1986).

8.8 Life History Synopsis:

Atlantic herring in the Northwest Atlantic reside from southern Labrador in Canada to Cape Hatteras, NC (Stevenson and Scott 2005). They spawn primarily on Jeffreys Ledge and Georges Bank between July and November, with peaks in August - September in the Gulf of Maine. However, recent research has suggested that the optimal spawning temperature window has shortened over the past 30 years (Kanwit and Libby 2009) (Molina 2025). Early life history development is inversely related to water temperature, with slower growth occurring at higher temperatures (Stevenson and Scott 2005). Eggs hatch after 6-15 days, and larval development time ranges from 4-8 months. Larvae absorb their yolk sac within 2.5 - 6 days, at which point the larvae become pelagic and can be transported away from or retained near spawning habitats, depending on prevailing current systems (Lough and Cohen 1982). Predation of eggs is linked to high mortality. Predators include haddock, cod, mackerel, winter flounder, moon snails, hermit crabs and starfish. Strong haddock recruitment has been linked to low abundances of larval herring (Boyar et al. 1973) (Burbank et al. 2023). Larval herring prey on copepods, with size depending on the size of the larvae (Sherman and Honey 1971). Juveniles form large schools in coastal waters (i.e. estuaries and embayments) throughout the Gulf of Maine in the spring and summer, and overwinter in deep bays or offshore (Stevenson and Scott 2005). Juveniles and adults are planktivorous and opportunistic feeders. As they grow and the size of their jaws increases, they consume larger organisms. Their diet therefore varies with season, their age and size, and location. (Stevenson and Scott 2005) (Sherman and Perkins 1971). A lack of availability of preferred prey in adults (due to strong mixing in the water column) can lead to suboptimal feeding and slow growth resulting in increased mortality from starvation and predation (Stevenson and Scott 2005). Adult herring make extensive seasonal migrations between summer spawning grounds on Georges Bank and in the Gulf of Maine and overwintering areas in southern New England and the Mid-Atlantic region (Stevenson and Scott 2005). There are two major spawning stocks in the Northwest Atlantic: the Gulf of Maine and Georges Bank/Nantucket Shoals. Stocks are separated based on larval distribution patterns, spawning times/locations, and growth rates (Iles and Sinclair 1982) (Boyar et al. 1973) (Anthony and Waring 1980). Both stocks are currently assessed in the U.S. as a single coastal stock complex managed by the New England Fishery Management Council. As of the most recent assessment (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2024), the stock is overfished but not experiencing overfishing based on the biological reference points for spawning stock biomass (SSB) and fishing mortality (F).
