32.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

Scup distributions in the spring and fall have shifted northward and offshore, which may necessitate new management strategies (Bell et al. 2015) (Palacios-Abrantes et al. 2023). Roday et al. (2026) analyzed fish distribution shifts using recreational catch and fishery independent data. Scup distributions showed a moderate range expansion but little to no change to center of distribution (Roday et al. 2026). Juvenile scup exposed to elevated pCO2 in a laboratory setting had no significant effect on growth, survival, or otolith condition. Scup spawning areas in Long Island Sound are already exposed to high pCO2, indicating some tolerance to ocean acidification (Perry et al. 2015).

32.8 Life History Synopsis:

Scup are found in the Northwest Atlantic from Cape Cod, Massachusetts to Cape Hatteras, North Carolina (Steimle et al. 1999). There are two U.S. stocks: Mid-Atlantic/New England (Atlantic coast) and South Atlantic (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2025). Spawning occurs once per year in coastal bays and estuaries in May-August, beginning with lower latitudes (Steimle et al. 1999). Eggs take 2-3 days to hatch depending on temperature. The larval stage lasts from 2 mm TL to 18-19 TL; the duration of this period is unclear. Larval distributions are highest in inshore bays and estuaries, suggesting that eggs and larvae do not travel far during planktonic stages (Steimle et al. 1999). The biggest cue for larval settlement is size (~25 mm), with size in part being a function of temperature - most common at 15-20°C (Steimle et al. 1999). Larval diets are poorly understood, but larvae have been successfully reared on zooplankton in lab experiments (Bowman et al. 1987). Early juveniles consume copepods, so larval scup may also consume copepods (Michelman 1988). Larval predators include plantivorous medusae, crustaceans, and fishes (Steimle et al. 1999). Juveniles have been caught in intertidal and subtidal habitats, over sand, silty-sand, shell, mud, mussel beds and eelgrass (Zosteria marina). Offshore winter-spring distributions indicate that they occur in habitats ranging from relatively flat, open, sandy-silty bottoms to the head of submarine canyons, and other areas with topographical relief and varying sediments. Juveniles can use biogenic depressions, sand wave troughs, and possibly mollusk shell fields for shelter in winter, suggesting structure may be important to juveniles (Able and Fahay 1998). Juveniles primarily feed on polychaetes, epibenthic amphipods and other small crustaceans, mollusks, and fish eggs and larvae (Steimle et al. 1999). Prey size increases with individual size, with copepods and mysids being important to early juveniles and bivalve mollusks consumed by larger fish (Bowman et al. 1987). Adult scup are also benthic feeders and forage on a variety of prey, including small crustaceans (including zooplankton), polychaetes, mollusks, small squid, vegetable detritus, insect larvae, hydroids, sand dollars, amphipods, and small fish (Steimle et al. 1999). Similar to juveniles, their prey size scales with individual size. Diets can vary based on location, and season. Feeding slows or stops entirely in winter (Byron and Link 2010). Adults are primarily benthic and utilize both estuarine and shelf benthos (Steimle et al. 1999). Age at maturity is around 2 years of age, with a maximum age between 15-20 years (Northeast Fisheries Science Center 2015). Scup undertake extensive inshore/offshore migrations driven by temperature preferences. They migrate south and offshore in the fall and return to inshore bays and estuaries in the spring to spawn. Scup can become inactive or dormant if caught in cold pockets < 6°C during their inshore spring migration and prefer temperatures 9-24°C (Steimle et al. 1999). The North Atlantic scup fishery is managed jointly by the Mid-Atlantic Fishery Management Council and Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission. The 2025 Management Track Assessment reported the Atlantic coast stock as not overfished and overfishing not occurring (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2025).
