34.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

Little information exists on the potential effects of climate on smooth dogfish abundance and distribution. Coastal areas, including estuaries and smooth dogfish nursing grounds, are affected by anthropogenic activities that degrade a portion of smooth dogfish habitat. These areas are also more likely to be impacted by hypoxia and changes to freshwater input/increases in turbidity (Plumlee et al. 2018). Smooth dogfish exposed to higher temperatures (increase of 6°C) did not result in a thermal stress response, indicating a possible tolerance and ability for range expansion (Bockus et al. 2020).

34.8 Life History Synopsis:

Smooth dogfish are a demersal, highly migratory shark species (HMS). The Northwest Atlantic population of smooth dogfish ranges from Cape Cod to Florida and the northern Gulf of America (Conrath et al. 2002). The species undergoes temperature-driven seasonal migrations north in the summer and south in the winter. They overwinter in Chesapeake Bay to South Carolina from January to May, migrating north to Southern New England beginning in early spring to give birth to pups (Castro 2010) (Dell’Apa et al. 2018). Females ovulate between June and July, with gestation lasting 10 months. Birth occurs in southern New England between early May to mid-July (Castro 2010). Once females have given birth in nearshore or estuarine areas,, young of year and immature age classes may remain in nursery grounds while females migrate north to suitable temperatures (Bangley and Rulifson 2017). Smooth dogfish prefer temperatures 9-32°C. They begin appearing in the mid-Atlantic and New England when temperatures are greater than 6-7°C, and are most abundant along the East Coast between 13-15°C (Bockus et al. 2020). Young of year individuals use shallow shoals and marsh creek habitats particularly during night hours. Juveniles are found throughout temperate coastal estuaries, creeks, and seagrass beds, which may serve as nursery grounds (Castro 2010). Juveniles prey on small shrimp, polychaete worms, and crabs, and may fill an important predatory role during times of year when other piscivores are rare (Rountree and Able 1996). Adult smooth dogfish are bottom feeders, primarily consuming crustaceans and mainly feeling at night. They are described as a trophic specialist due to the morphological and functional modifications that allow them to feed on hard-shelled/exoskeleton-bearing prey (Castro 2010) (Bangley and Rulifson 2017). Smooth dogfish are prey items for a variety of larger sharks and fishes (Gelsleichter et al. 1999). Age at maturity is 2-3 years for males and 4-7 years for females, with a maximum age of 11 years for males and 13 years for females (Conrath et al. 2002) (Giresi et al. 2013). In state waters, smooth dogfish are managed by the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission’s Interstate Fishery Management Plan for Coastal Sharks (Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission 2024). NOAA Fisheries produces an annual Stock Assessment and Fisheries Evaluation (SAFE) Report detailing the status of HMS in federal waters (National Marine Fisheries Service 2023). The current stock status is not overfished and overfishing is not occurring (Southeast Data, Assessment, and Review 2015).
