45.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

Yellowtail flounder abundance, distribution, and recruitment have been associated with a number of environmental and climate indices. Rapid warming of the Northwest Atlantic has resulted in decreased productivity in yellow flounder (Kittel et al. 2026). Higher temperatures can increase mortality in yellowtail flounder larvae, and faster rates of warming causing earlier stratification may reduce settlement rates in juveniles (Klein et al. 2017). Yellowtail recruitment in Southern New England is highly correlated with the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) and air temperature (Sullivan et al. 2005). Low recruitment since 1990 was associated with unfavorable environmental conditions caused by a northward shift in the Gulf Stream (Xu 2016) (Kittel 2025). The Mid-Atlantic Cold Pool, bottom temperature, and the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO) are also linked to recruitment (Kittel 2025). As warming continues, joint management between the United States and Canada may be more challenging as distributions change (Sumaila et al. 2020).

45.8 Life History Synopsis:

Yellowtail flounder are found in the Northwest Atlantic from Newfoundland to Chesapeake Bay, inhabiting sandy bottom waters between 130-230 m depth (Johnson et al. 1999) and at temperatures of -1 to 18°C (Overholtz and Cadrin 1998). Previous research has suggested that fish live up to 17 years, possibly as high as 20 years, although fish older than 7 years is unusual (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2012). The fishery is divided into 3 stocks: Gulf of Maine/Cape Cod, Georges Bank, and Southern New England/Mid-Atlantic (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2025a) (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2025b) (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2025c). Most females are mature by ages 2-3 (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2012). Spawning occurs from mid-February to July along the continental shelf waters of Georges Bank, northwest of Cape Cod, Southern New England and nearshore along New Jersey and southern Long Island (Martin and Drewry 1978). Spawning peaks from April to June in southern New England and late June to July in Canada (Berrien and Sibunka 1999). Laboratory studies show eggs begin to hatch 6-7 days after fertilization (Benoit and Pepin 1999). At sea hatching times range from 14.5 days at 4⁰C to 4.5 days at 14⁰C (Walsh and Morgan 2004). Eggs are pelagic (most at 10-90m depth), with movement limited to currents in spawning locations (Smith et al. 1975). Larvae move from the surface down to about 20 m, with larger larvae making longer migrations. This habit may assist in the dispersal of larvae because circulation at the surface transports the larvae further and is likely associated with feeding behavior (Smith et al. 1978). Larvae feed on zooplankton, particularly Calanus (Kittel 2025). Once larvae reach metamorphosis size (11.6-16 mm) bottom temperature (4-8°C) and depth (41-70m) are the most important parameters for determining settlement (Collette and Klein-MacPhee 2002). Juveniles utilize marine and estuarine sandy and muddy substrates, as well as estuarine and marine water columns (Collette and Klein-MacPhee 2002). Rotifers, copepods, and brine shrimp nauplii were fed to juveniles in laboratory studies (Benoit and Pepin 1999). Calanus copepods are considered a critical food source for larvae/juveniles (Purchase et al. 2000). Amphipods (41% by weight) and polychaetes (38.5%) dominate the diet of adult yellowtail, but they also can consume cnidarians, small crabs and shrimps, mysids, cumaceans, isopods, univalve and bivalve mollusks, echinoderms, and sometimes small fish (Bowman et al. 1987) (Bowman et al. 2000). Similar to other groundfish, yellowtail may be impacted by increasing grey seal and dogfish populations and resulting increased predation pressures (Kittel 2025). Other predators of juvenile and adult yellowtail include spiny dogfish, skates, cod, hakes, and monkfish (Johnson et al. 1999). Minimal migration patterns show that Southern New England yellowtail traveled eastward during spring and summer and westward in the fall and winter as a result of seasonal changes in temperature (Smith et al. 1975). Recent tagging studies, however, show that individuals generally don’t move between stocks (Goethel et al. 2015). Yellowtail flounder are managed by the New England Fishery Management Council through the Northeast Multispecies (Groundfish) Fishery Management Plan. The Georges Bank stock is jointly managed by the Council and Canada, as the stock extends beyond the international boundary. The Cape Cod/Gulf of Maine stock is not overfished and not subject to overfishing (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2025a). The Georges Bank stock is overfished but under a rebuilding plan; overfishing status is unknown (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2025b). The Southern New England/Mid-Atlantic stock is overfished but under a rebuilding plan, but not subject to overfishing (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2025c).
