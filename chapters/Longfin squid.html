25.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

Cephalopods have been shown to be sensitive to environmental variability and climate change, due to their short lifespan (Moustahfid et al. 2021). Inshore squids, such as longfin, may have complex responses to climate change. As temperatures increase, squids will likely hatch smaller and earlier, grow faster, mature at a smaller size, and have shorter life spans. They may also require more food and oxygen, but a reduced capacity to cope without food (Pecl and Jackson 2008). Longfin squid recruitment may negatively be affected by impacts of increasing ocean acidification on the paralarval stage (Arkhipkin et al. 2015). Longfin squid raised under elevated pCO2 increased hatching time (~24 hours longer) and had shorter mantle lengths as well as significantly reduced statolith surface area (by 25%) and abnormally shaped and porous statoliths (Kaplan et al. 2013). Abundance and distribution changes have been linked to the North Atlantic Oscillation, Gulf Stream, and surface fronts on the outer continental shelf (Dawe et al. 2007) (Hendrickson et al. 2023) (Manderson et al. 2011).

25.8 Life History Synopsis:

In the Northwest Atlantic, longfin squid are most abundant from Georges Bank to Cape Hatteras although their range expands from Newfoundland to the Gulf of Venezuela (Jacobson 2005). The North Atlantic stock undertakes seasonal migrations: moving offshore in the fall to overwinter along the shelf edge and slope and migrating inshore in the spring following warming water temperatures to spawn (Hatfield and Cadrin 2002). More recent studies after the development of statolith aging techniques highlight a peak in spawning from late spring to summer (Hatfield and Cadrin 2002) (Macy and Brodziak 2001) and hatching in late summer (Jacobson 2005). Some spawning occurs throughout the year both inshore and offshore depending on hatching month and length-at-age (Macy III 1994) (Macy and Brodziak 2001), with winter spawning thought to occur primarily south of Cape Hatteras (Hatfield and Cadrin 2002). Eggs incubate for 11 - 27 days on sandy or muddy bottoms attached to solid substrate, with faster development times are warmer temperatures. The duration of the larval stage is unclear, but they occupy coastal surface waters. Once juveniles, they transition from inhabiting surface waters to a demersal lifestyle (Jacobson 2005). The Gulf Stream and warm core rings may facilitate the recruitment transport of larval and juvenile squid northward, but potential for inputs to the population from the Southeast U.S. Continental Shelf and offshore are difficult to quantify (Hatfield and Cadrin 2002). Juveniles and adults are generally demersal and inhabit the continental shelf and upper slope to depths of 400 m. This varies seasonally, with adults occupying shallower habitats in summer and fall, and deeper habitats in the winter. Diel vertical migrations have been reported (Jacobson 2005). Juveniles consume planktonic organisms, primarily copepods. Their preferred prey type varies with size (Jacobson 2005). Larger individuals feed on crustaceans and small fish. Diets shift seasonally from crustacean-dominated in the spring to fish-dominated in the fall. Cannibalism is observed in individuals larger than 5 cm (Jacobson 2005). Longfin squid are prey for a variety of finfish, marine mammals, and birds (Jacobson 2005). Most statolith-aging studies suggest that longfin squid have a short lifespan of around 1 year or less, reaching maturity shortly before spawning and senescence (Macy and Brodziak 2001). Longfin squid are managed by the Mid-Atlantic Fishery Management Council under the Mackerel, Squid, and Butterfish Fishery Management Plan. The stock is not overfished, and overfishing reference points are not available (Mid-Atlantic Fishery Management Council (MAFMC) 2025).
