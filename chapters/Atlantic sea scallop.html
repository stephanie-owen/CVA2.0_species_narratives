11.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

The Atlantic sea scallop population in the Northwest Atlantic is subject to climate-related environmental stress from rising temperatures and ocean acidification (Zang et al. 2022). Scallop habitat can be degraded by trawling/dredging activity (Maguire et al. 2002). Scallop feeding behaviors are temporarily impacted by noise pollution associated with offshore wind construction (J’ez’equel et al. 2022). Changes in temperature and food availability are likely the predominant drivers for reduced growth in sea scallops (Cranford et al. 1998). Rheuban et al. (2018) modeled the Atlantic sea scallop fishery under a range of climate and management scenarios and found the possibility of reduced biomass up to 50% by the end of this century. Results of a scope for growth model with increased warming food deficiency indicated a range contraction of suitable sea scallop habitat in the Mid-Atlantic Bight (Zang et al. 2022). A second scope for growth model by Zang et al. (2023) investigated the relationship between sea scallop size and temperature. Under future climate warming, sea scallops exhibited a range contraction and decline in maximum shell height. An integrated assessment model simulating sea scallop population dynamics with oceanographic and socioeconomic influences indicates that sea scallop harvests could experience a substantial decline by 2050 under increased CO2 emissions (with the assumption that ocean acidification decreases recruitment and slows growth and warming increases growth) (Cooley et al. 2015).

11.8 Life History Synopsis:

Atlantic sea scallops are one of the most economically important fisheries in the Northwest Atlantic, with average annual ex-vessel revenues of over $500 million (Zang et al. 2022). Their habitat ranges from the Gulf of St. Lawrence to Cape Hatteras, North Carolina (Stewart and Arnold 1994) with primary fishing areas in Georges Bank, the Great South Channel, and the Mid-Atlantic Bight (Hart and Rago 2006). After a period of severe overfishing in the 1970s to 1990s, regulations including effort reductions, gear restrictions, and closed areas were introduced to rebuild the stock (Hart and Rago 2006). Spawning occurs in late summer or early fall, although smaller spawning events at other times of the year are possible (Culliney 1974). Gametes from other scallops, agitation, or tides may trigger scallop spawning (Parsons et al. 1992). Larvae reside in the water column for 5-7 weeks (Thouzeau et al. 1991) and circulate with residual currents ranging from 6-25 cm/s (Culliney 1974). Larval exchange is influenced by duration and depth of planktonic drift, gyre strength, and weak cross-isobath flow (Thouzeau et al. 1991). The transition to the benthos of the freeswimming larvae occurs at a size of about 0.25 mm shell height and is accompanied by drastic changes in diet, morphology, and locomotory ability (Bourne 1965). Mean fall temperatures impact the time larvae take to settle (Tremblay et al. 1994). Sea scallop larvae are potentially preyed on by filter feeders and larval and adult planktivores (Tremblay and Sinclair 1986). Juvenile Atlantic sea scallops are mainly found on gravel, small rocks, shells, and among branching animals and plants that permit attachment of juveniles (Thouzeau et al. 1991) and feed on suspended particles, primarily phytoplankton and microzooplankton (Shumway et al. 1987). Adult sea scallops are filter feeders on phytoplankton and other suspended organic particulates from the water (Shumway et al. 1987). Adult sea scallops prefer temperatures of 5-18°C (Hart and Chute 2004). Predation on juvenile and adult sea scallops decreases with shell height; common predators include Atlantic cod, wolffish, sculpins, winter and yellowtail flounder, as well as boring polychaetes, sea stars, crabs, and lobsters (Hart and Chute 2004). The New England Fishery Management Council manages Atlantic sea scallops in federal waters under the Atlantic Sea Scallop Fishery Management Plan. As of the 2020 Management Track Assessment, the stock is not overfished and overfishing is not occurring (Northeast Fisheries Science Center 2020).
