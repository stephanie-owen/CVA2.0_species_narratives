16.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

Projected surface temperatures in the Gulf of America may alter the spatial and temporal extent of Atlantic bluefin tuna spawning. Model results from (Muhling et al. 2011) indicate decreased larval bluefin tuna occurrence in late spring in the Gulf by 39-61% by 2050 and 93-96% by 2100, with early spring spawning becoming more prevalent. Erauskin-Extramiana et al. (2026) also modeled decreased habitat suitability for spawning in the Gulf of America. They predict a poleward shift in Atlantic bluefin tuna distribution and greater spatial overlap with prey in higher latitudes. Nursery habitats in the Gulf of America are impacted by oil spills. Upwards of 5% of predicted spawning habitat within the U.S. Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ) was impacted by the Deepwater Horizon oil spill (Hazen et al. 2016).

16.8 Life History Synopsis:

Western Atlantic bluefin tuna are a highly migratory species observed from Newfoundland to the Gulf of America; they occupy temperate surface waters but can dive to depths of 1000m (Fromentin 2006). Spawning occurs between spring and summer throughout western North Atlantic spawning grounds, namely the Gulf of America (March-June) and Slope Sea (June-September). Spawning in the Slope Sea occurs one to two months later than the Gulf of America due to differences in sea surface temperatures (Baglin 1982). Bluefin tuna eggs hatch after 2 days and larvae remain pelagic for 3-4 weeks (Shropshire et al. 2022). Larval bluefin tuna are found near spawning grounds and dispersed via wind and the margins of ocean currents. Edges of ocean currents and eddies provide access to warmer sea surface temperatures and nutrient-rich waters that support larval foraging of zooplankton prey while avoiding predation (Shropshire et al. 2022). Favorable habitat for bluefin larvae in the Gulf of America was determined to be moderately warm waters (23.5-28.5°C) in the eddies of the Loop Current, and outside of cooler, higher chlorophyll continental shelf waters (Teo, Boustany, and Block 2007). Along the coast of the southeastern United States, larval stages may benefit from nearshore meanders of the Gulf Stream that can produce upwelling of nutrient rich water along the continental shelf edge (Shropshire et al. 2022). Larvae initially feed on zooplankton and progress to a piscivorous diet at a relatively small size (Llopiz and Hobday 2015). Starvation is the largest cumulative source of mortality in early larval stages, and predation on older larvae is a main source of mortality in later stages (McGowan and Richards 1989). Juvenile habitats are located over the continental shelf in the summer and further offshore in the winter. Juvenile life stages follow similar seasonal movements compared to adult life stages, though are generally more restricted to coastal areas (National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) 2006). Juvenile and adult bluefin tuna are opportunistic, generalist predators feeding mainly on zooplanktivorous fishes and crustaceans. Predation of larger fish continues with increasing body size, with juveniles moving from sand lance to mackerel and herring as adults (Logan et al. 2011) (Logan, Golet, and Lutcavage 2015). Adult bluefin tuna migrate seasonally from winter to early summer spawning grounds in the Gulf of America, Straits of Florida, and Slope Sea to summer and fall foraging grounds throughout the Gulf Stream, South and Mid-Atlantic Bight, Gulf of Maine, Nova Scotia Shelf, and east of the Flemish Cap (Block et al. 2001). Age at 50% maturity estimates for Atlantic bluefin tuna vary between 5 to 12 years, with a maximum age of over 30 years (Standing Committee on Research and Statistics (SCRS) 2021). NOAA Fisheries, with recommendations from the International Commission for the Conservation of Atlantic Tunas (ICCAT), manages the Atlantic bluefin tuna fishery in the United States through the Atlantic Highly Migratory Species Management Division. As of 2023, the stock is being managed ICCAT Recommendation 21-10. Population numbers are unknown but the stock is not subject to overfishing (International Commission for the Conservation of Atlantic Tunas (ICCAT) 2022).
