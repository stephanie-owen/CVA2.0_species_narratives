14.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

Black sea bass in the Mid-Atlantic Bight are currently expanding their distribution northward into the Gulf of Maine (McMahan, Sherwood, and Grabowski 2020). Black sea bass in their newly-expanded range have a less diverse diet, lower condition, and a younger age-at-maturity than their counterparts at the historic northern range of the stock (McMahan, Sherwood, and Grabowski 2020). A laboratory study on Mid-Atlantic black sea bass observed a decline in bodily processes that require oxygen demand above 24°C, suggesting black sea bass will continue to shift their range poleward with warming conditions (Slesinger et al. 2020). Tabandera et al. (2024) found that winter bottom temperature was positively correlated with recruitment deviations in black sea bass. Black sea bass in the southern Mid-Atlantic Bight also appear to occupy a smaller portion of their habitat in years with an increase in cold shelf water on the shelf slope. Northward distributions of black sea bass are resulting in new species interactions and habitat overlaps. In Narragansett Bay, black sea bass juveniles now share nursery habitats with juvenile tautog and cunner, however dietary overlap was size-dependent and ontogenic diet shifts eventually led to niche separation (Bell et al. 2015) (Taylor et al. 2023).

14.8 Life History Synopsis:

Black sea bass are found along the U.S. Atlantic coast from Cape Cod to the Gulf of America in structured habitats such as reefs, shipwrecks, or oyster beds (Drohan, Manderson, and Packer 2007). In the Mid-Atlantic Bight, spawning occurs from April to October, beginning first in the southern portion of their range and northern latitudes starting later in the year (Kenneth W. Able and Fahay 1998). Early larval stages last approximately 72 hrs at 21°C but can last anywhere from 2-6 days depending on temperature (Berlinsky et al. 2004). Afterwards, larvae enter a 21-24 day planktonic phase where they can disperse up to 50 miles by currents (Watanabe et al. 2021). Eggs and pelagic larvae are vulnerable to predation, especially by gelatinous zooplankton (Arai 1988). Larvae settle in nearshore shelf habitats and then migrate to estuarine nurseries to settle and metamorphose into juveniles (occurs at 13-24mm) (K. W. Able, Fahay, and Shepherd 1995). Larvae are abundant at temperatures between 13-21°C and leave estuaries for deeper water when temperatures dip below 14°C (Kendall 1972). Larvae consume microalgae and zooplankton, including rotifers (Richards 1963). Metamorphosis is likely the biggest factor influencing settlement (Musick and Mercer 1977). Structural complexity appears to be an essential component of juvenile black sea bass habitat offshore as well as inshore nurseries throughout the species range. In offshore areas, recently settled fish occur in accumulations of shell on sand substrata, complex microtopographies on exposed clay, on rocky reefs, and on wrecks (K. W. Able, Fahay, and Shepherd 1995). Primary black sea bass settlement habitats are probably located along the near shore continental shelf in accumulations of the shells of bivalves, including Atlantic surfclams (Spisula solidissima) (K. W. Able, Fahay, and Shepherd 1995). Juvenile growth rates impact winter mortality. Smaller juveniles have smaller lipid reserves and higher metabolic rates and therefore face higher predation or starvation risks in the winter (Geissinger et al. 2021). Juveniles, which are diurnal, visual predators, prey on benthic and epibenthic crustaceans (isopods, amphipods, small crabs, sand shrimp, copepods, mysids) and small fish. As they mature, their diet diversifies to include crabs, krill, isopods, shrimp, and small fish (Kimmel 1973). Adult black sea bass perform seasonal inshore/offshore migrations between coastal spawning grounds and offshore overwintering habitats. Migrations are triggered by declining temperatures (around 10-12°C) and begin in the North moving southward (Tabandera et al. 2024). In the Mid-Atlantic Bight, individuals have home ranges as large as 7km2 (Tabandera et al. 2024). Observations suggest that spawning aggregations consist of a single dominant male and a harem of females. These spawning aggregations may rely on visual cues rather than chemical or environmental cues (Tabandera et al. 2024). Black sea bass are protogynous hermaphrodites, with fish changing sex from female to male as they increase in age and size (McGovern et al. 2002). Age of sexual transition varies with latitude with females maturing and undergoing sexual transition at greater ages in northern latitudes (O’Brien, Burnett, and Mayo 1993). On average, age at maturity in the Mid-Atlantic is 2-3 years but can extend up to 5 years (O’Brien, Burnett, and Mayo 1993). Maximum age for females is 8 years and 12 years for males (Northeast Fisheries Science Center 2017). The black sea bass fishery in the Northwest Atlantic is divided into two stock subunits (north and south) and is managed by the Mid-Atlantic Fishery Management Council along with the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission. As of the 2025 Management Track Assessment, the Mid-Atlantic stock is not overfished or subject to overfishing (Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission (ASMFC) 2025).
