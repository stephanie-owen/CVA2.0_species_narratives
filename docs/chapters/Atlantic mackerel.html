9.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

Atlantic mackerel are sensitive to temperature changes, particularly temperatures greater than 5°C (Overholtz, Hare, and Keith 2011). From 1968 to 2008, the distribution of mackerel in the Northwest Atlantic has shifted roughly 250km north and east in addition to changes in depth distribution to more shallow on-shelf waters. These changes in position and depth are known to be correlated with the gradual warming of the Northwest Atlantic (Overholtz, Hare, and Keith 2011). Atlantic mackerel egg distribution has also shifted northeastward as a consequence of warming, depleting egg production in important spawning locations in southern New England (Richardson et al. 2020). Bruge et al. (2016) projected changes in Northeast Atlantic mackerel spawning distribution under future climate scenarios, and found that the spawning center of gravity is expected to shift westward and northward. Mbaye et al. (2019) investigated effects of environmental conditions on Atlantic mackerel spawning habitat in the Gulf of St. Lawrence. Generalized additive model (GAM) results showed that spawning habitat in the Gulf of St. Lawrence could expand and extend to western and southern Newfoundland.

9.8 Life History Synopsis:

Atlantic mackerel in the Northwest Atlantic inhabit cold and temperate continental shelf waters from the Gulf of St Lawrence to Cape Lookout, North Carolina (Studholme 1999). Spawning occurs from April to August in the Northwest Atlantic and progresses from south to north with warming temperatures and following migration patterns (Sette 1943). There are two spawning contingents: the southern contingent spawns from April to July in the Mid-Atlantic Bight and Gulf of Maine, and the northern contingent spawns in the southern Gulf of St. Lawrence in June and July (Berrien 1982) (Morse, Fahay, and Smith 1987). Temperature is a cue for spawning. Spawning does not occur until water has warmed to 8°C with most egg production occurring between 9-14°C (Sette 1943). More recent work has shown that mackerel spawning and migration timing is negatively correlated with sea surface temperature (Jansen and Gislason 2011). Early life stage development happens quickly, with eggs hatching in 4-7.5 hours, depending on temperature (Berrien 1982). Larvae (< 13 mm) occur primarily in offshore waters from Chesapeake Bay to southern Gulf of St. Lawrence (Sette 1943). Larval and juvenile depth varies diurnally and with age (Collette and Klein-MacPhee 2002). Larvae feed primarily on zooplankton and copepods, and larvae > 6.4 mm are known to experience cannibalistic behavior (Castonguay et al. 2008). Juveniles rely on water column habitats, both on the shelf, and in estuaries (Guyant et al. 2022). Juveniles and adults are opportunistic feeders that prey on a wide range of crustaceans, copepods, planktonic organisms, and fishes (Sette 1950). Squid, seabirds and larger piscivores, including Atlantic cod, prey on juvenile mackerel (Studholme 1999). Adults over winter in moderately deep water, 70-200 m, along the continental shelf. After migrating inshore in April-May, they form the two spawning aggregations (Berrien 1982). Predators of adult mackerel include larger fish, sharks, skates, seabirds, and marine mammals (primarily whales and seals) (Studholme 1999). Atlantic mackerel are managed by the Mid-Atlantic Fishery Management Council under the Mackerel, Squid, and Butterfish Fishery Management Plan. As of the 2025 assessment, the stock is not overfished and overfishing is not occurring, but is rebuilding to target levels (Northeast Fisheries Science Center 2025).
