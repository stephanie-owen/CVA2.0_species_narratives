17.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

There has been a documented shift in bluefish distribution, spawning, habitat use in the Mediterranean (Sabatés, Martín, and Raya 2012) (Villegas-Hernández, Lloret, and Muñoz 2015). Bluefish distributions in the Northwest Atlantic seem to be shifting north and east (Tyrell et al. 2022). Increasing fall sea surface temperatures may extend spawning season further into the calendar year (Tyrell et al. 2022). If warming alters spawning phenology, there are concerns that juveniles may arrive in estuaries before their prey. Seasonal mismatches have been shown to impact growth, body condition, and reproductive success (Taylor and Able 2006). Changing distributions of prey species as a result of climate change may alter interactions between bluefish and their prey (Whitfield 2026).

17.8 Life History Synopsis:

Bluefish inhabit temperate and tropical surface waters in the North Atlantic from Maine to the Atlantic coast of Florida (Shepherd and Packer 2006). Bluefish in the North Atlantic are a single, migratory stock with two discrete spawning periods. The first occurs during March-May near the edge of the continental shelf of the South-Atlantic Bight. The second occurs between June and August in the Mid-Atlantic Bight (Shepherd and Packer 2006). Eggs hatch after 46-48 hours at 18 - 22.2°C to pelagic larvae (Deuel, Clark, and Mansueti 1966). Larvae spawned in the South Atlantic (spring-spawned cohort) are subject to advection north via the Gulf Stream and inshore via warm core rings. Eggs in the southern part of the Mid-Atlantic Bight may be advected south and offshore (Shepherd and Packer 2006). Transport of larvae and/or juveniles across the shelf- slope region may be aided by wind-driven surface flow (Hare and Cowen 1996). Sherman et al. (1984) note that wide distribution of bluefish larvae over the shelf reduces their vulnerability to the predator field and environmental perturbation. Larvae prey on copepods (Shepherd and Packer 2006). Peak abundance of larval bluefish in Southern New England and the Mid-Atlantic Bight coincided with zooplankton production (Sherman et al. 1984). Changes in prey availability and distribution can impact bluefish condition and reproductive success (Taylor and Able 2006). Juvenile bluefish inhabit estuaries, bays, and coastal oceans with sand, oyster beds, and submerged aquatic vegetation habitats (McBride and Conover 1991). Juveniles enter estuaries when temperatures are greater than 13-15°C and leave when temperatures fall below 10-12°C (Smith, Berrien, and Potthoff 1994). Juvenile migration into estuarine habitats appears tied to a shift in eating other fish. In estuaries, adults and juveniles eat whatever taxa are locally abundant (fish, crustaceans, shrimp, and polychaetes). In ocean habitats, young-of-the-year bluefish switch to piscivory predation with increasing size, similar to estuarine habitats (Marks et al. 1996). Bluefish are opportunistic foragers, and the composition of adult bluefish diet depends largely on the available prey, but they consume similar prey items as juvenile bluefish (Shepherd and Packer 2006). Bluefish have long been recognized as a notable predator of forage fish (Tyrell et al. 2022). Adult bluefish conduct seasonal migrations following warmer waters, overwintering in the south and moving north in the spring and summer (Shepherd and Packer 2006). Migrations may be cued by photoperiod as well, and have been observed to shift northward in warm years (Nye et al. 2009). Shortfin mako sharks are identified as a major predator of bluefish and may consume more individuals than removed by the fishery (Wood et al. 2009). Most bluefish are mature by age 2, with approximately half maturing by age 1 (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2015). The maximum age for bluefish in the North Atlantic is 12 years (Salerno, Burnett, and Ibara 2001). In the Northwest Atlantic, bluefish are managed by the Mid-Atlantic Fishery Management Council and Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission. The last stock assessment occurred in 2025, where bluefish were found to not be overfished nor is overfishing occurring (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2025).
