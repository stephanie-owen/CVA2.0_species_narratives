42.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

Percent biomass of windowpane flounder in the Northwest Atlantic increases with changes in seasonality, from cold springs, to warm springs, to fall (Dunn, Moxley, and Halpin 2016). Increasing temperatures (> 7-10°C) have been shown to displace windowpane flounder (Bell et al. 2021). In New York/New Jersey Harbor, cold winter temperatures decreased windowpane abundance and overwinter mortality in small juveniles (Wilber et al. 2016). Overwintering success appears to be driven in part by temperatures, with extreme cold conditions resulting in high overwinter mortality of age-0 individuals. This likely has a larger impact on fall-spawned juveniles since resilience to extreme cold temperatures is size-dependent and fall-spawned individuals have less time to grow (Neuman and Able 2009). Individual windowpane have also demonstrated avoiding hypoxic conditions (Cadrin et al. 2021).

42.8 Life History Synopsis:

Windowpane flounder inhabit shallow waters from the Gulf of St. Lawrence to Florida, but are most abundant from Georges Bank to Chesapeake Bay (Chang et al. 1999). They reach maturity around 1.5 years of age and can live up to 8 years (Cadrin et al. 2021). Windowpane spawn from February to November on the inner continental shelf, Georges Bank, and within estuaries. Spawning occurs in the Mid-Atlantic Bight in both spring and fall, whereas spawning in Georges Bank peaks in summer (Able and Fahay 2010) (Cadrin et al. 2021). Windowpane spawn in the evening or at night on or near bottom at temperatures ranging from 6-21°C (Chang et al. 1999). Eggs hatch after 8 days. The duration of the larval stage is unknown, but eggs and larvae remain in the water column feeding on suspended copepods (Chang et al. 1999). Surveys from Canadian waters suggest that small (< 10 cm TL) windowpane consume bay shrimp and crab larvae, which may be a transition point from zooplankton prey to larger prey items (Hanson and Wilson 2014). Larvae settle to the bottom at approximately 10 mm TL. However, individuals collected on Georges Bank may be planktonic up to 20 mm. There is no indication that settlement is driven by environmental factors (Morse and Able 1995). Juveniles that settle in shallow inshore waters move to deeper offshore waters as they grow. Juveniles inhabiting Georges Bank (< 60 m) undergo seasonal movements to deeper waters along the southern flank of the Bank occur during late autumn, as bottom temperatures drop, and overwintering occurs in deeper areas until late spring (Klein-MacPhee 2002). From Massachusetts to the Gulf of Maine, they can be found in estuaries and on the shelf (Chang et al. 1999). Adults are found on the benthos in waters < 100 m and tolerate a wide range of temperatures (0-26.8°C) (Chang et al. 1999). Adult windowpane may travel along the coast for considerable distances. Juveniles and adults may migrate to nearshore or estuarine habitats in the Mid-Atlantic Bight. Seasonal movements into offshore overwintering habitats have also been documented (Chang et al. 1999). Stomach content data collected during Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) bottom trawl surveys indicate windowpane feed on small crustaceans (e.g., mysids and decapod shrimp) and various fish larvae including hakes and tomcod, as well as their own species (Langton and Bowman 1981) (Chang et al. 1999). Windowpane flounder are managed by the New England Fishery Management Council under the Northeast Multispecies (Groundfish) Fishery Management Plan. The fishery is divided into two stocks: Northern Gulf of Maine/Georges Bank and Southern New England/Mid-Atlantic. Overfishing and overfished status of the northern stock is unknown, as the assessment has not estimated reference points since 2020 (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2023a). The southern stock is not overfished and overfishing is not occurring (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2023b).
