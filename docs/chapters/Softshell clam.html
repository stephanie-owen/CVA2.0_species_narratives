35.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

Softshell clams are sensitive to increasing temperatures; temperature influences growth and reproduction, and their southern range boundary is controlled by sea surface temperatures (V. Kennedy and Mihursky 1971). Population declines in softshell clams in the Wadden Sea result from thermal stress caused by high water temperatures (Freitas et al. 2007). Softshell clams are likely to be vulnerable to ocean acidification, impacting their behavior, physiology, growth, and survival (Clements and Hunt 2023). Koo et al. (2017) described salinity accounting for the majority of spatial variation in softshell clam growth in Plum Island Sound, Massachusetts. Salinity gradients in Northwest Atlantic estuaries are expected to alter with sea level rise and increasing precipitation, which may prompt management concerns (Koo et al. 2017).

35.8 Life History Synopsis:

The softshell clam is a circumboreal species native to the Northwest Atlantic from Labrador to Cape Hatteras (Newell, Hidu, and Parsons 1986). Softshell clams reach maturity in 1-4 years, depending on the length of each growing season. The typical lifespan is between 10-12 years, but cases have been recorded of some softshell clams reaching 28 years of age (Cohen 2005). These clams can be found at temperatures as low as 2°C but prefer between 10-28°C (Newell, Hidu, and Parsons 1986) (Landry 2021). Spawning occurs between 4-25°C, with optimal temperatures between 10-15°C (Abraham and Dillon 1986). Fecundity also increases with size and age, particularly with females (Cohen 2005). Separate male and female sexes occur in the species, with fertilization occurring externally in the environment. Female softshell clams can release from 1-5 million eggs in their lifetime and typically one to two spawnings happen a year, one in early spring and the other in late summer, depending on locality and monthly tidal cycles (Abraham and Dillon 1986). Approximately 12 hours after fertilization, larvae hatch from the eggs. Larvae spend 2-5 weeks free-floating with plankton and other organic particles before metamorphosizing and settling to the bottom of the sea floor. The clams then spend another 2-5 weeks moving along the sea floor or temporarily attaching themselves to the surface of objects (Cohen 2005). Food availability and optimal oceanographic conditions to help transport and/or retain eggs and larvae in suitable habitats help drive local recruitment (Bowen and Hunt 2009). Eggs and larvae are preyed upon by a variety of predators, including fish and other filtering invertebrates. High fecundity likely reduces the impact of predation on egg and larvae (Newell, Hidu, and Parsons 1986). Metamorphosis and settlement to the benthos can be delayed in favor of more suitable substrates, and may be driven by chemical cues (Snelgrove, Grant, and Pilditch 1999). Once settled, softshell clams spend 2-5 weeks moving along the sea floor or temporarily attaching themselves to the surface of objects. They begin to burrow into the sediment in brackish waters, estuaries, and marine habitats, and stay burrowed for the rest of their life (Cohen 2005). Burrows are constructed 20-30cm below the sediment surface and in waters up to 200m depth. Sandy sediment, with a sandy-mud mixture or with some gravel, is preferred (Tyler-Walters 2003). The softshell clam is an active suspension feeder, filtering organic particles and microinvertebrates such as plankton, filamentous algae, diatoms, jellyfish and fish larvae, and flagellates (Tyler-Walters 2003). Predation plays a large role in juvenile and adult distribution and survival. Predators include birds, fish, shrimp, worms, crabs, and snails (Newell, Hidu, and Parsons 1986). The green crab, an invasive species, is one of the more destructive clam predators and declines in clam production have been linked to increases in green crab abundance. Blue crabs are also major predators in Chesapeake Bay; exclusion studies have illustrated that recruitment and survival increases when blue crabs are excluded (Landry 2021). The softshell clam supports important state-managed commercial and recreational fisheries, and are harvested with rakes, forks, or hydraulic dredges (Newell, Hidu, and Parsons 1986). Recent declines in commercial landings can be attributed to overfishing, habitat loss, pollution, and climate change. However, mariculture in softshell clams has been successful in building populations for restoration or commercial fishing (V. S. Kennedy and Beal 2023).
