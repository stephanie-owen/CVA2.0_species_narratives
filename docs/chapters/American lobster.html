4.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

The Northwest Atlantic has been warming at an exceptionally fast rate (Saba et al. 2016), which has contributed to declines in fecundity and population size in Southern New England (Goldstein, Zarrella-Smith, and Pugh 2022) while the fishery in the central Gulf of Maine has increased dramatically (Le Bris et al. 2018). Temperatures have increased in the lobster’s inshore range, and evidence suggests that the productivity of the stock is severely compromised with increased thermal stress and disease (Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission (ASMFC) 2025). The assemblage of predators of American lobster has shifted with warming conditions in the Gulf of Maine, with benthic and demersal predators, such as black sea bass (Centropristis striata) and spiny dogfish (Squalus acanthias) becoming increasingly present throughout the lobster’s range, taking the place of traditional predators, such as Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua). The effects of invasive and range-expanding species may also lead to greater predation risk for juvenile lobsters (Goldstein et al. 2025).

4.8 Life History Synopsis:

American lobsters inhabit the coastal and oceanic waters of the Atlantic Ocean from Labrador to North Carolina. They are widely distributed within most of the coastal waters in the Northeast U.S. with some major offshore concentrations along the outer edge of the Continental Shelf and upper slope between Georges Bank and Delaware Bay (Mackenzie and Moring 1985). Spawning takes place during a 4 month period from late May through much of September, when females leave their solitary shelters about 7 days before molting to share the shelter of a dominant male (Factor 1995) (Mackenzie and Moring 1985). Lobster larvae are planktonic for 6-8 weeks (Factor 1995) and travel from surface waters offshore to inshore areas of Southern New England and the Gulf of Maine. Larval distribution is affected by the distribution of spawning females and by surface current velocity and direction, temperature, salinity, light intensity, and hydrostatic pressure (Katz, Cobb, and Spaulding 1994) (Harding and Trites 1988) (Hudson and Fradette 1993) (Mackenzie and Moring 1985). Larval survival is likely affected by pollution of the marine environment by human activity, especially coastal waters (Connor 1972). There is an inverse relationship between temperature and the time from hatching to the postlarval stage (when they settle). Larvae take as few as 11 days at 22°C and as many as 54 days at 10°C to reach the postlarval stage. It is speculated that thermoclines are a negative cue for larvae/post larvae settlement (Factor 1995) (Templeman 1936). Juvenile lobsters utilize shelters (rocks and vegetation) in the benthic habitat, and may also burrow in mud (Wahle and Steneck 1991). Juveniles are omnivorous but also scavenge for food. Much of their diet consists of bottom invertebrates, crabs, urchins, mussels, polychaetes, periwinkles, and sea stars, but they may also consume fish and plants (Lawton 1987). Adult lobsters are also considered omnivores and scavengers . However, their diet is dominated by mollusks (especially mussels), echinoderms, and polychaetes (Elner and Campbell 1987). Juveniles and adults prefer bottom water temperatures of 8-14°C (Mackenzie and Moring 1985). Inshore, adult lobsters appear to have a limited home range of about 2.2 km, however offshore lobsters tend to migrate greater distances (up to 345 km in 71 days) (Mackenzie and Moring 1985). American lobster are managed by the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission as three stock units based on life history differences: Gulf of Maine, Georges Bank, and southern New England (Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission (ASMFC) 2025).
