21.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

Cobia off the eastern U.S. Atlantic coast currently overwinter offshore in waters south of North Carolina. Suitable habitat increases in the summer months with warming temperatures. Habitat distribution models suggest increased habitat suitability northward, shifting distributions poleward, and possible earlier seasonal migrations (Crear et al. 2020). This shift northward could prompt revision of management regulations as the fishery expands to new states or regions. Early life stage cobia may be somewhat resistant to projected rises in CO2 levels (Ebanks 2013), although juvenile cobia in a laboratory study exhibited poor growth performance and reduced feeding at elevated temperatures (Le et al. 2024).

21.8 Life History Synopsis:

Cobia are a pelagic, continental shelf fish distributed from Massachusetts to Argentina but are most abundant from Virginia to the Gulf of America (Shaffer and Nakamura 1989). Two stocks are recognized in the United States: one along the Atlantic coast south to Georgia and the other along the Atlantic coast of Florida into the Gulf (SouthEast Data, Assessment, and Review (SEDAR) 2020). Cobia are batch spawners, and move inshore to spawn when temperatures reach 20-25°C. They spawn over a period of 4-6 weeks, peaking in June-July in Virginia (Joseph, Norcross, and Massmann 1964). Cobia eggs hatch within 36 hours of fertilization. Given the short time frame between fertilization and hatching, it is likely that eggs and larvae do not travel far from spawning grounds (Shaffer and Nakamura 1989). Little is known about the feeding habits of larval and juvenile cobia. Adult cobia are generalist carnivores, feeding on benthic-epibenthic crustaceans (Portunids and Callinectes sp.), teleosts, cephalopods, and elasmobranchs (Smith 1995). Juveniles are pelagic and often associated with structure for feeding and shelter. Juveniles and adults can be found near shipwrecks, buoys, shellfish beds, and submerged aquatic vegetation (SouthEast Data, Assessment, and Review (SEDAR) 2020). Few predators of cobia in U.S. Atlantic waters are known, although dolphinfish (Coryphaena hippurus) have been reported to prey on small cobia (Shaffer and Nakamura 1989). Over 50% of cobia are mature by age 2, and can live up to 15 years (SouthEast Data, Assessment, and Review (SEDAR) 2020). Adults are highly migratory. Cobia exhibit a general migratory trend of overwintering in south Florida and the Florida Keys waters, and then migrating north along the Atlantic and Gulf coasts in late winter-early spring. They likely return to south Florida coastal waters in the late fall (Hendon, Franks, and Fulford 2008). The Atlantic migratory cobia are managed by the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission. The population is not overfished and overfishing is not occurring as of the last benchmark stock assessment in 2020 (SouthEast Data, Assessment, and Review (SEDAR) 2020).
