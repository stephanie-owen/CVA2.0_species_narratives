13.7 Climate Effects on Abundance and Distribution:

Atlantic wolffish is a cold water species and has been termed a “temperature seeker” (Kulka et al. 2011). Early life history stages and fish near the southern stock boundary may be the most vulnerable to surface water temperatures exceeding 9-11°C (Rountree 2002). Juvenile Atlantic wolffish exposed to temperatures of 15°C exhibited reduced growth and higher metabolic rates compared to those at 10°C (Hinchcliffe et al. 2025). In the North Sea, Atlantic wolffish have shifted their distribution and decreased abundance at the southern end of their range and in shallow waters (Bluemel et al. 2022). Bianucci et al. (2016) observed a connection between low dissolved oxygen and contraction of the Atlantic wolffish population on the Scotian Shelf. Model results of future dissolved oxygen conditions also extend to areas with high Atlantic wolffish biomass (Bianucci et al. 2016). Similar results were also observed in (Brennan, Blanchard, and Fennel 2016) which predicted declines in dissolved oxygen concentrations and increases in temperature outside of known adult wolffish tolerances.

13.8 Life History Synopsis:

Atlantic wolffish inhabit deep, rocky waters of the Northwest Atlantic from Labrador to the Great South Channel and Georges Bank (Rountree 2002). In the Northwest Atlantic, wolffish are believed to spawn primarily in autumn (Rountree 2002). Spawning is likely temperature dependent (Atlantic Wolffish Biological Review Team 2009). Wolffish will migrate to colder temperatures before the spawning season, and egg laying and guarding behavior could be disrupted if bottom temperatures increase above 10°C (Collette and Klein-MacPhee 2002). However, laboratory experiments suggest that timing may be highly plastic and dependent on local conditions, including photoperiod (McBride et al. 2022). Spatial and temporal variation in spawning may be contributing to the stock’s regional reproductive isolation (Atlantic Wolffish Biological Review Team 2009). Eggs are deposited in rocky dens and incubation time is highly dependent on water temperature, varying between 3 - 9 months (Atlantic Wolffish Biological Review Team 2009). After hatching, prolarvae stay close to the bottom. Larvae consume a broad range of amphipod, crustacean, and fish larvae (Rountree 2002). The prolarval stage can last from a few hours to 6 days (Collette and Klein-MacPhee 2002). The larvae become pelagic shortly after and stay within the water column from 20 days to 2 months, with colder temperatures prolonging the larval period, before migrating back to the bottom (Rountree 2002). Juvenile and adult wolffish occupy the bottom habitats of the continental shelf and slope, using large stones and rocks for shelter and nesting and soft substrates for feeding (Atlantic Wolffish Biological Review Team 2009) (Collette and Klein-MacPhee 2002). Juvenile wolffish primarily consume echinoderms (Atlantic Wolffish Biological Review Team 2009). Adults prey upon hard-shelled invertebrates such as mollusks, crustaceans and echinoderms. Stomach contents have included sea urchins, whelks, cockles, sea clams, brittle stars, crabs, scallops and other shellfish in addition to an occasional redfish (Atlantic Wolffish Biological Review Team 2009) (Rountree 2002). While previous studies have suggested that wolffish migrate inshore/offshore seasonally for spawning or maturity, it is unclear if this is a true migration or if behavioral changes are affecting catchability as eggs are found both inshore and offshore. More recent tagging studies have illustrated that movements in the Northwest Atlantic are limited, but the species has been shown to migrate in the Northeast Atlantic (Rountree 2002) (Simpson et al. 2015) (Gunnarsson et al. 2019). Spiny dogfish, sea raven and Atlantic cod are the dominant predators for juveniles. For adults, the occurrence of predation is low and is not likely to be having a significant effect at the population level (Atlantic Wolffish Biological Review Team 2009). The maximum reported age for wolffish in the Gulf of Maine is 33 years (Fairchild et al. 2015). Most individuals reach maturity by age 6, but this may vary regionally due to temperature (Rountree 2002) (McBride et al. 2022). Atlantic wolffish are managed by the New England Fishery Management Council under the Northeast Multispecies (Groundfish) Fishery Management Plan. As of the 2022 Management Track Assessment, the stock is not overfished and overfishing is not occurring (Northeast Fisheries Science Center (NEFSC) 2022).
